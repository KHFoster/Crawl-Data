Ammunition folks have been somewhat busy the past couple of years. Entering the ammo business a couple of years ago, they started with a few assorted pistol calibers. Now, they offer dozens of loadings including .223, .308, .300 Win Mag, and yes, 300 AAC Blackout. Heck, they’re even making revolver ammo like .38 Special, .357 Magnum, .44 Special, .44 Magnum and even .45 Colt. I don’t recall seeing too many Sig Sauer .45 Colt revolvers at my local range, so apparently the ammo team isn’t limiting themselves to Sig guns.</p><p><a href="https://www.gunsamerica.com/blog/test-sig-sauers-new-300-blackout-match-subsonic-ammo/" target="_blank">We tested the Sig Sauer 300 Blackout Subsonic ammo</a> a while back, so we figured it was time to report on the <a onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/outgoing/www.sigammo.com/sig-blk300/sig-300blk-supersonic/');" href="http://www.sigammo.com/sig-blk300/sig-300blk-supersonic/" campaign="SigSauerElitePerformance300BlkSupersonic" target="_blank" class="clickTracking">300 Blackout 125-grain Supersonic load</a>. Sig also makes an expanding 120-grain hunting round called the HT, but we’ll cover that in a future story.</p><p>The supersonic load is match grade and is tipped with a 125-grain Sierra Matchking projectile. Technically, like most Sierra Matchking rifle bullets, it’s a hollow point, or more accurately, Open Tip. However, that’s more of a manufacturing convenience and accuracy aid, and it’s not intended to expand, nor will it. These bullets are meant to fly straight and stay together. This supersonic round features a pointed meplat projectile design to help ensure maximum velocity retention. The intended result is flatter trajectories and more energy on target.</p><p>I shot a goodly supply of this ammo through a Daniel Defense DDM4v5 300 Blackout rifle. It’s got a 16-inch barrel and has proven itself to be an accurate shooter, so I figured it would give me a decent indication of the consistency of the Sig Supersonic Blackout load. Sig loads ammo using a tray system, so 210 rounds are processed at once for each step of the loading process. They’ve designed and built all sorts of consistency and quality checking technology into their process that makes use of laser, optics, scales and human eyeballs to check and verify every dimension and weight throughout the process. Where that attention to detail should become apparent is on the range, so I did some velocity and accuracy testing.</p><div id="attachment_56992" style="width: 1034px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"><a onclick="javascript:pageTracker._trackPageview('/downloads/blog/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/Sig-Sauer-300-Blackout-Manufacturing-1.jpg');" href="https://gastatic.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/Sig-Sauer-300-Blackout-Manufacturing-1.jpg" class="lightbox"><img class="wp-image-56992 size-large" src="https://gastatic.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/Sig-Sauer-300-Blackout-Manufacturing-1-1024x683.jpg" alt="Sig also uses match grade brass for the 300 Blackout loads." width="1024" height="683" srcset="https://gastatic.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/Sig-Sauer-300-Blackout-Manufacturing-1-1024x683.jpg 1024w, https://www.gunsamerica.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/Sig-Sauer-300-Blackout-Manufacturing-1-150x100.jpg 150w, https://www.gunsamerica.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/Sig-Sauer-300-Blackout-Manufacturing-1-400x267.jpg 400w, https://www.gunsamerica.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/Sig-Sauer-300-Blackout-Manufacturing-1-768x512.jpg 768w, https://www.gunsamerica.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2016/05/Sig-Sauer-300-Blackout-Manufacturing-1.jpg 1200w" sizes="(max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px"></a><p class="wp-caption-text">Sig also uses match grade brass for the 300 Blackout loads.</p></div><p>First, I set up a Shooting Chrony Beta Master Chronograph 15-feet down range to check velocity and consistency. I fired 10-shot strings so I could get some meaningful extreme spread and standard deviation data. My overall average velocity worked out to be 2,074.8 feet per second from the DDM4v5. The slowest round moved at 2,031 feet per second while the fastest clocked at 2,115, so the extreme spread was 84 feet per second. If you do the fancy math, standard deviation worked out to 23.14.</p><p>I had a 50-yard range available, so I also fired five-shot groups to get an indication of accuracy from this particular rifle and ammo combination. Again, this ammo showed great consistency. Out of the Daniel Defense DDM4v5 rifle, I got groups in the .6-inch range. I was using a Trijicon ACOG 300 Blackout optic with fixed 3x magnification, so precision was a bit limited by the sight picture.
